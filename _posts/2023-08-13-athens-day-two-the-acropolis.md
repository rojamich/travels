We woke bright and early to get a jump start on the long lines into the Acropolis and to get ahead of the anticipated 96 degree heat. We had a filling, fresh, and refreshing breakfast and set off to get a good spot in line. From cruise day zero til the end of our Athens visit we religiously applied sunscreen. Often twice a day! We were grateful to have packed our travel sunscreen this time.

  
We made it to the Acropolis entrance at about 730 Am and it opens at 8. We were sixth in line! What a deal!

The Acropolis has been the "heart of Athens" since 7000BC! Filled with temples, the main four are the Parthenon, Erechthenion, Propylaea, and Temple of Athena Nike.

  


 The Odeon of Herodes Atticus, this ampitheater seats 5000 and is still used today for performances! - It originally had a roof but I imagine the starlit sky would be more impressive here.

An empty pedestal. Each victor over Athens has placed their statue here. Marc Antony, General Agrippa, and Eumenes II / Attalus II.

Our next audiotour spot took us to the Erechtheion which was the place where Athena and Poseidon fought for naming rights to Athens. Poseidon threw his trident, opening a gash in the Earth which brought Athens the gift of water. Athena stabbed a rock with her spear and sprouted an Olive tree- Greece has more than 100 million olive trees! Thus Athena won, don't worry the gods are still friends.

The joke told was "If Poseidon won the naming contest, it wouold be Poseidonia, if Zeus won, it would be Zeustralia, and if Hades won, it would be....hot as hell!" Which, at times, I'm sure it does feel that way there. Alas, the city was named Athens after the winner, Athena. 

  


  
The last stop on our tour took us to the place of the Greek flag. Greece's flag holds nine strips of blue and white which symbolizes the nine syllables of Greek phrase for "Freedom or Death".

Apparently, when Nazis occupied Athens, they confronted a Greek soldier and demanded he take down the flag. He calmly removed it, wrapped himself in it, and jumped to his death.   
  
One month later, two teens scaled the wall here, removed the Nazi flag, and rehung the Greek flag, this plaque was constructed in their honor.

It was rest time after a long hot day of sightseeing but we would be back later that evening for the National Archaeological Museum!  
We ate some average food, showered, and rested up for that evening. It was much hotter on our walk over to the Museum and we were grateful to reenter the air conditioning (although they keep the doors open so it wasn't super effective).

 

Rick Steve's was there once again to walk us through some of the sights- for which we were extremely grateful! He pointed out things we would have never noticed like the trends that sculptured art took at the time such as beginning with stiff and crude works of art, moving into highly emotional after emerging from the dark ages, then the barely noticable smile indicating inner happinss.    
  
In the 500-450BC timeframe it was the "severe style of artwork" where expressions were serious.   
There were museum employees everywhere, watching to ensure no one poses with the statues or touches them. The art was out without protective glass.    
  
We noticed a trend as we stopped to listen to our audio tour. We would stand, looking at a piece of art, off by ourselves. Rick and Lisa would explain in our headphones what the statue was said to depict, the history of the time period, etc and then people would come up. Almost every time. They would stop, look at the sculpture for a bit, look at us, try to read the Greek-only inscription, look back at the sculpture, consult each other, and wander off confused. Again and again. It seed like they were asking...what are they looking at??   
  
We FINALLY found an Athena statue here. Apparently, Athena loved snakes as they shed their skin and are representative of renewal. However, she slayed Medusa...which seems contradictive lol
