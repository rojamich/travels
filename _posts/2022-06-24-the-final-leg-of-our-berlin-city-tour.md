In the middle of the square sits a glass window you can look down into. If you look down through the glass you see a simple but moving memorial, a room filled with empty bookcases. It is a memorial to the event where staff and students threw over 20,000 banned booked into a fire. Its very hard to see through the glass but look in the upper right-hand corner of the following photo.

The plaque nearby it says, “Where they burn books, in the end, they will also burn people. This was written in 1820. Pretty prophetic, I think.

  


The next memorial was dedicated to unknown soldiers and unknown concentration camp victims. It depicts a mother holding her dead son. There is a hole in the top of the building to let in light. I think the memorial would be especially moving when it rains.

We made it onto Museum Island which is exactly what it sounds like. Berlin has a little island in the city that has many bridges to allow visitors to pass onto it to tour the many museums there. 

The Berlin Cathedral is also housed here and it is huge! You also get a sneak peek at the Berliner on the right of the cathedral! Their storm drain covers here are really neat! Look at all the sight to see in Berlin! We learned on the tour (at least I did) that one in every ten bombs that landed here, did not explode. The soil is too soft. So there are signs on the rivers that say “Do not drop anchor here”. They find unexploded bombs all over the city during construction.

Something cool we learned during the tour was that the crosswalk lights are historic! They actually tried replacing them but the citizens fought back and actually filed a lawsuit. These little crosswalk people cost the city a pretty penny but are now a representative of Berlin. And look how cute they are!

We ended the tour with a statue of Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels (as they both studied here at Humboldt University) that sits in a park directly across from the Berliner. We had an appointment two days later to head to the top of it! The Berliner is a 1200 foot TV Tower and was built to in 1969 to celebrate the 20th anniversary of Communist East Germany.
