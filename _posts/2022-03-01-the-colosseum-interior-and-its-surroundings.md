After completing photo time, we realized no one was in line to see the Colosseum yet – it was about 820am and they didn’t open til 9am. We were expecting our longest line yet! But nothing yet, so we rushed around the area looking for headphones. After about four stops we found a shop with headphones, only to see they were only iPhone compatible. Oh brother. We would have to make do without them. 

    We headed back to the Colosseum, where a crowd had begun to gather. I was paying close attention to when they were going to let people in so they beckoned us over and we bolted to the entrance! We were the first ones into the Colosseum that day. After hearing all the horror stories about how busy it is at all times, I never thought I would see the day! I thought you had to be there at 7am for that kinda action!

 The colosseum was more than I imagined it could be. While I had seen the outside before, the inside was staggering. Hearing the history behind it, made me appreciate it even more! We loved the Rick Steve’s Audio Tour and all the sound effects they had added uniquely to this one! Understanding how the stadium once looked during its heyday was really interesting. The stadium floor was no longer there and you could see the underground maze that allowed the gladiators and animals to be led up a pully system and on stage. I guess before I just thought they led them on from somewhere in the stadium but the reality is much more practical! Mike got us access to the arena floor which gave us an intimate view of the underground passages. We were able to sit here and imagine the history of the gladiators and the Colosseum as we listened to our audio tour.

The tour even explained why the whole colosseum is pock-marked. The building was pilfered for its iron, many times over. They used it to build and decorate other buildings throughout the city. The colosseum was also used to house squatters at various times in history.

From the upper levels of the Colosseum we can see the Arch of Constantine which is a symbol of Triumph. He later legalized Christianity. Prior to this, it was illegal to be a Christian and you were likely tortured or killed in a brutal fashion (or both). After, it was illegal not to be a Christian, and the same punishment continued for non-Christians. Here is a shot I got of it, a few mornings later when it was virtually empty!

Just across the street we see the Roman Forum, a popular meeting place in ancient times that houses basilicas, temples, the house of the vestals, and a whole lot more! One of our favorite stories was the history behind the statues that once stood here of two sisters, Venus and Roma. Now we see the remnants of the Temple of Venus and Roma. Roma Amor, a palindrome said to date back to ancient times.
