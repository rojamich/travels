The metro here was less than desirable and I’d avoid it if I could. Not due dangers but because its unreliable and gentrified. 

    We got to the Vatican about 40 minutes before it opened and a line had already begun to form. We were about 50th in line. By the time 9am struck, there were about 100 people waiting to enter. As we followed the tour, we saw things like the large pine cone statue which is a symbol of fertility! The next statue depicts the tragedy during the Trojan War. Snakes were sent down by the Greek Gods to punish the man and his two sons for attempting to warn others about the Trojan Horse deceit. The last is a beautiful view of Roma.

The following photos represent some of our favorite highlights of the museum. The first is the Pope’s study, as painted by Raphael Sanzio. He used modern-day brilliant minds to depict ancient Greece’s brilliant minds. For example, Plato was painted in the image of Leonardo da Vinci. I think its genius to design a study to be filled with an inspirational mural filled with bright minds studying. The next photo was the view from the museum that Mike was really excited to be able to see! The third photo is another example of ornate beauty displayed by the church. Its astonishing what they could do with the ceiling décor. The only thing I’ve had on my ceiling is popcorn-style stucco.

As we stepped into the Sistine Chapel, we were immediately snipped at by guards, “No photos!” There’s a huge mural that spans across a football-sized ceiling and you don’t expect people to try and take photos? Where’s the giant sign that should alert people _before_ they get there?! We settled into a good spot where we could have a view of the whole room and listened to Rick Steve explain what we were looking at. Mike was a little rebel and snuck a photo anyway.
