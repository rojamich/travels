The next morning, we woke early and headed to the Dachau Concentration Camp Memorial Site. I didn’t take any photographs because it just didn’t feel right to. It was something you had to feel to experience, not see. We had to make a few connections to get there but we were able to get an audio guide through the memorial site that was fairly helpful in explaining what we were looking at. They also had labels to tell you what was going to be a part of each exhibit so you could choose to skip it or not.

Upon entering the grounds, you could feel a huge weight sitting on your shoulders. The photographs of the victims lined up in the square awaiting torture, starvation, humiliation and/or all three were harrowing. Going through each exhibit, you had this awful pain growing in your chest. Seeing the belongings taken from the victims with their future to be unknown was downright awful. Never having known about the experiments they performed on prisoners; it was very eye-opening for me. The worst part was standing inside the fake showers that were actually used as gas chambers. There must have been so much confusion, heartbreak, and fear in that room. 

Leaving was a relief. Just the pit in your stomach and thoughts of the innocence remained with us. 

We recovered during the bus and metro rides back. It took awhile to get out there which allowed us to work through the thoughts that were haunting us and talk about what we had seen.
