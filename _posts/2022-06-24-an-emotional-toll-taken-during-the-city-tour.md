We continued walking to the Memorial to the Murdered Jews of Europe. Here stand 2,711 coffin-shaped figures that represent the six million Jews that were killed by the Nazis. The title was intentionally straight-forward as Germany wanted to show they were taking accountability for their actions in this time period. The memorial is both humbling and heart-breaking. The stones vary in height. Some are quite small while others dwarf you.

We weaved through this symbolic graveyard, turning this way and that. Trying to take in everything without letting it take us. With an exhausted sigh of relief to have made it out of that emotional trap, we walked on to Hitler’s bunker. The bunker no longer stands and was destroyed a few times over. There was talk of preserving it but much of the history in Berlin was either erased or carefully represented so as not to encourage the neo-Nazi movement. We read and listened about Hitler and other Nazi leaders of the time, killing their families and then themselves.

We stopped into a nearby Italian restaurant for an early lunch to get warm and console our heavy hearts. It had been a morning full of sadness. Thankfully the tour lightens up a bit from here. 

Our next stop would be back at Unter Den Linden. Which means under the Linden trees. Rick Steve led us in the metro tunnel to see the history that remained. There were photos of how the city looked and even original signage.

The next statue is of a King of Prussia, Frederick the Great. Prussia, at the time, was known at the time to be very militaristic. Voltaire said “Whereas some states have an army, the Prussian Army has a state” which I thought was really interesting! He was a very interesting King being that he was smart, military wise as well as able to play the flute, spoke six languages and successfully ruled Prussia!
