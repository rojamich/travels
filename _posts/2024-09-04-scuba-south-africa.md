We were scheduled to do a refresher dive to go over some skills before diving again. The recommended dive requires a max of six months since you last dove and we had dove just over that in Costa Rica so we were grateful for the opportunity to get familiar with the gear again. 

We were lucky to have Taryn as our dive instructor because she was so full of energy, excitement, and personality! She recommended we try something called [Biltong](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biltong) \- similar to jerky but softer and juicier! It came in many flavors and I liked the spicy one called Piri Piri. We found out the water temperature would be 57 degrees this day and it would be a dive with a 7 mil (very thick wetsuit), a hood (see dorky photo), and gloves/boots. 

For reference, a dive in Cozumel, Mexico where we were able to skin dive (no wetsuit, just swimsuit) was about 78 degrees for water temp.

Nevertheless, we were excited to get some diving in.

  


It was frigid in the morning and the exposed facial skin was shocked. We got moving around and it helped a lot. We were able to warm up in the sun while preparing for our set of dives on the boat after our refresher dive. No photos of actual diving since the battery for our gopro gave out just before our trip :(

  


Our group of 8 headed out on a tiny boat on what appeared to be calm water. As we got further and further out into the bay we started hitting some waves and we tried not to let the nerves settle in. Mike and I get VERY seasick. Over the counter seasickness meds have not worked but the scopolomine patches have worked wonders for us in the past thankfully! It was at this moment we spotted a couple of seals playing/diving into the water and it was cute to watch them being so playful. Moments later we spotted a group of penguins swimming around! I never thought I would see them naturally this way and it really sank in how appreciative I am to have this experience. I sat there and just took a few moments to notice how grateful I am for this life, Michael and his support helping me out of my comfort zone, and for this grand adventure. It was such a significant moment of peace for me.

We got to know our dive master Robyn on the journey out to sea and she was so much fun as well! Her and Taryn were a fabulous combo and eaisly my favorite dive guides thus far.

We arrived at the first dive location: Whittle Rock and felt the full magnitude of the boat rocking. 6 out of 8 people on the boat were nauseous and 2 actually threw up. We got into the water as quickly as possible with our second backwards entry into the water [(Here is a video if you want to see how it works)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tDwT23h8OOY), which is really fun and I personally think looks cool hahaha.

 As we began to decend I saw my first jellyfish on a dive! It was a teeny tiny purple/translucent fellow and I admired it while we sank into the depths. The visibility wasn't marvelous but it was enough to see schools of fish and the most vibrant purple-colored reef I have ever seen! It was a shade of purple I have never experienced before! I wasn't chilly while we dove because the current was strong enough that I was working hard to move. Typically, I don't register that I'm even swimming when we dive because it's a relaxed, slow-moving swim. However, I was kicking strong to keep up with Robyn.

  
We ascended and I immediately felt seasick. Thankfully, the guides took us to shore for lunch and the time needed to decompress before heading out on a second dive.    
  
Robyn and Taryn chose a divesite closer to shore that offered more protection from the current. On our way back to shore we were mindblown when a whale came to the surface and spouted! We have never seen whales in person (outside of a zoo/aquarium) and this was completely unexpected! We actually ended up seeing more various marine life at the surface than we did underwater during this experience XD.

After stomach settling and warming up on land, we headed out to our second dive spot: Castle Rock. The current had worsened now in the afternoon and we, once again, descended as quickly as possible into a kelp forest - a first for Mike and I! I didn't expect much other than seeing some kelp but it was actually really magical looking (here is what a kelp forest looks [like](https://www.news.uct.ac.za/article/-2017-03-15-kelps-in-southern-africa-are-thriving-but-some-key-inhabitants-of-kelp-forests-are-not)). The current was so strong that we were immediately separated from the rest of the group (3 of us and the secondary dive master) and were tossed about. It was the most challenging dive we have ever experienced (roughly 25 dives prior to this). I had never been seasick underwater before, it was so rough. As we finally ascended, a seal circled us curiously and playfully and we were grateful to be rewarded that way!  
More divers were seasick at the top and we were whisked back to land.

Despite the conditions, we were grateful to dive, try a seriously cold dive(for the experience), and to learn what we do not enjoy lol.  
I say that because Galapagos Islands are a dream destination for me - especially for diving. But one dive particularly has been described as expert level only due to the conditions/currents. I have gone back and forth on whether or not it would be worth it for the marine life viewing. I am fairly confident now, that it is not but I would be happy to experience other dives in the area.    
  
That night we found a doner spot in Bo-Kaap and enjoyed turkish doner :) I added tzatziki sauce to my fries and I really enjoyed it! Then we relaxed in the hotel steam room and sauna before crashing for the evening.    
  
One thing I didn't mention before was that Cape Town has security everywhere! There is security stationed at (literally) every other block with uniforms and reflective vests and very friendly attitudes.
